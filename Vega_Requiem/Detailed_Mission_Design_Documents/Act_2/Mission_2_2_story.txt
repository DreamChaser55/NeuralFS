I have been writing these logs since I took command of the $f{ Amadeus $}. Seventeen months. Most of
them are logistical. Maintenance schedules, personnel reviews, fuel consumption notes - the
administrative debris of commanding a ship. I read back through them sometimes, when sleep won't
come, and I am struck by how little of what I remember is in them.

Tonight I'm writing something different.

$R{ The Amadeus cannot make the transit to Antares. $}

I have known this for approximately six hours, since Lieutenant Commander Pak brought me the
engineering assessment and then stood very still while I read it. The drive coupling failures
are not repairable in the field. The structural damage to the aft sections has compromised the
subspace alignment lattice. We could, theoretically, attempt the jump. Pak estimates a
forty-three percent chance the ship survives transit to $y{ Antares $}. Fifty-seven percent chance the
jump $R fails catastrophically mid-transit, which means no ship, no crew, no data, nothing.

I will not take those odds with nine hundred and twelve people aboard.

I spent two hours after that looking at alternatives. I am experienced enough to recognize when
I am doing this not because I expect to find something, but because I need to have looked.
There is nothing. The $h{ Ahriman $} is cutting off the $y{ Deneb $} and $y{ Beta Aquilae $} approaches. The $y{ Capella $}
node has had $h{ Shivan $} capital assets for five days. We can't be towed - no tow asset with
sufficient capacity is anywhere near this system.

The $f{ Amadeus $} holds position. The crew evacuates. The ship absorbs whatever the $h{ Ahriman $} directs
at her while the pods get clear.

I will remain on the bridge.

I want to explain why, because I think there is a version of this that looks like theater - the
Admiral going down with his ship because it is expected of him, because someone wrote it in a
regulation somewhere, because he lacks the imagination to conceive of a different choice. That
is not what this is.

The $f{ Amadeus $} needs someone at her helm to be useful in the time she has left. The bridge crew
will evacuate in the last wave. Someone has to be at the controls until then, directing the
turrets, adjusting the $f{ Amadeus $}'s aspect to give the pods the best possible cover. That someone
is me. I am the most qualified person remaining aboard for the job and the most expendable
individual in terms of what the withdrawal operation needs to succeed.

I want to be clear-eyed about this. My life is worth the cover it provides.

What I will tell you - what I am writing down because these logs are transmitted to Command and
I believe someone will read them eventually - is that I am not afraid. I have spent forty-one
years in uniform. I have been at war for most of them, first the Vasudans, now this. I have made
decisions in that time that I cannot revise and would not if I could, and decisions I regret
acutely, and most decisions that were simply the best available choice in circumstances that
offered no good choices.

The $f{ Amadeus $} is a fine ship. She has a fine crew. Those pods will reach $y{ Antares $}.

One more thing. $f{ Alpha $} wing is out there. Whoever is flying $f{ Alpha 1 $} - I don't know which of the
new pilots drew the senior slot, but they've been doing this for six days and they haven't died
yet, which at this point I consider a meaningful credential. I need them focused on the pods and
not on the $f{ Amadeus $}. The $f{ Amadeus $} is not the objective. People are the objective.

I am going to finish this log and then I am going to go to the bridge and sit in my chair and
do my job until I can't anymore.

It has been a privilege to serve.

Admiral Chen Wei

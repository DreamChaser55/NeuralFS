THE APPROACH VECTOR
A fiction viewer narrative — displayed before Mission 1-3: "No Truce With the Tide"

Sub-Legate Khoresh had been watching the jump point for eleven hours when the PVD Vigil made her
transit into the Vega system.

The Vigil came through at 0412 standard, trailing the three Isis transports that Sub-Imperator
Hethor had designated Theta wing — the refugee carriers, evacuating PVN administrative staff
from a forward outpost that no longer had anything to administer. Khoresh logged their arrival
with the care that characterized everything he did, noting the exact time and the Vigil's hull
percentage and the configuration of Theta wing's docking manifests. He had been Third Navigation
Officer for six years and had the kind of mind that catalogued details because the alternative
was to stop cataloguing and start thinking about other things.

The other things, in this case: Vasuda Prime had been bombarded. The home system was falling.
Four billion dead, the last communication had said, and then the communications had stopped.

He kept cataloguing.

"Any Terran traffic in system?" Sub-Imperator Hethor asked from the command tier, not looking
up from the tactical display.

"Negative, Sub-Imperator. GTA transponders are showing the Amadeus group somewhere near the
inner system, but we're reading no fighters on Vega Prime's patrol vectors." Khoresh paused.
"Vega Prime's defense net is not responding to our IFF requests."

"It may be destroyed."

"Yes, Sub-Imperator."

She considered this with the stillness that Khoresh had come to understand meant she was
calculating something rather than feeling nothing. Hethor had been in command for three years
and had fought the Terrans for two of them. She was forty-three and had the kind of face that
had been young once and stopped being young with deliberate intent.

"Plot an intercept to the Capella node," she said. "If the enemy has entered through there,
that is where we will find them."

"Sub-Imperator." This was Legate Sovan, tactical officer, at the station to Khoresh's left.
"Our approach vector was filed with 9th Fleet Command twelve hours ago. Standard protocol."

"Yes," said Hethor. "And?"

Sovan was quiet for a moment. "The Hammer of Light has assets in this system. They monitor
Command traffic on open channels when they can obtain the cipher keys." Another pause. "The
cipher keys for the 9th Fleet were compromised at Vasuda three weeks ago. We filed a flag
but replacements have not been issued."

The bridge was very quiet for a moment.

Hethor looked up from the tactical display. "Adjust our approach vector," she said. "Take
us in at fifteen degrees off the standard corridor. Reduce speed to point-six standard."

"That will add forty minutes to our intercept time," Khoresh said, already calculating.

"Yes." She looked at him with something that might, in a different face, have been called
uncertainty. "It will also give us forty minutes of data before we commit to the node approach.
If there is something waiting at the Capella node for us—" She stopped.

"We will have forty minutes of warning," Khoresh finished.

"Better than none," Hethor said, and turned back to her display.

It was not enough. Twenty minutes into the adjusted approach, the signatures began to appear
on long-range passive sensors — positioned at the Capella node as if they had been waiting
there for some time, arranged in an intercept pattern that assumed the standard approach vector.

That was when Khoresh understood that the forty minutes had been purchased not because it would
save the Vigil, but because it might save Theta wing.

"All ships," Sub-Imperator Hethor said, with the voice she used for orders she did not expect
to hear countermanded, "break formation on my mark. Theta wing: make for the Antares node.
Do not wait for us. Do not look back."

Khoresh entered the navigation change into his console. His hands were steady. He had catalogued
the shape of the trap, and it was a good trap, and there was no way out of it for the Vigil.

But Theta wing was already turning toward Antares.

He went back to his log and wrote the time.
